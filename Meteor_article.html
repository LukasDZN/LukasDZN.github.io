If a meteor falls, but no one is around to see it...

April 13th, 2020

This is a little, mostly visual analysis I've done on a dataset from NASA that provided meteor: latitude, longtitude, category of being seen or found, mass and year of occurence.
Full project and repository.

Meteors we have recorded

It is interesting that the observation rate grew exponentially but uniformly, even though no modern technology was present for hundreds of years prior. There have still been ~100 reported cases around 1800's.

'Scuse me, how much do you weigh? (meteor mass distribution)

There have been a few dozen cases, where meteors anywhere from 1 to 60 tonnes have been documented, however, we can observe that most are quite tiny. So, let's zoom in a little bit:

The absolute majority of meteors seem to be tiny, anywhere from a few grams to a few hundred.

Note that the amount of observed meteors (red) hasn't really changed.

First we notice a sharp increase in recorded observations in a very short period of time around 1974 (specifically - meteors that we're found), which started a whole new trend of documentation, meaning that, perhaps, some better tools or means of identification of extraterrestrial substance were discovered. This trend consistently continues up until the very latest year. Furthermore, a lot smaller meteors began being documented as well.

Conclusion

In the end, the areas that have:
- Higher population density
- Non-humid and clear skies
- Infrastructure for reporting
Have a lot higher amount of records, however, we know that meteors hit earth rather uniformly (except for a slight difference around the poles), therefore, we can probably assume that the amount of small impacts (the ones that can't be traced from a distance via satellites or observatories) is uniform throughout earth AND as dense as the densest impact point on the map.
